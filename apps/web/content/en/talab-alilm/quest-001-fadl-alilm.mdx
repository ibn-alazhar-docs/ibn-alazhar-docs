## Introduction

Knowledge in Islam is not merely incidental learning or intellectual luxury — it is an act of worship, a means of drawing closer to Allah, and a pillar of the faith itself. The sacred texts are replete with exhortations to seek knowledge, elevations of the status of scholars, and declarations that the superiority of the learned over the devout is like that of the moon over the stars.

This document offers a foundational perspective on the virtue of knowledge and its pursuit, serving as an entry point to the collection on the fiqh of seeking knowledge and its methodology.

## The Virtue of Knowledge in the Qur'an

The Qur'an abounds with verses that elevate knowledge and its people. Among the most prominent is His saying: ﴾Say: Are those who know equal to those who do not know?﴿ [Az-Zumar: 9]. This verse establishes a fundamental distinction between the learned and the ignorant, making knowledge a criterion of distinction.

Also His saying: ﴾Allah will raise those who have believed among you and those who were given knowledge, by degrees﴿ [Al-Mujadilah: 11], where He couples the scholars with the believers and singles them out for elevation. The expression "by degrees" indicates that knowledge has levels and ranks — a notion that points directly to the fiqh of priorities, which will be treated in a later document.

<div class="callout callout-info">

**Reflection:** Allah did not say "the scholars are raised exclusively" but linked it to both faith and knowledge, indicating that beneficial knowledge is that which is coupled with sincere faith.

</div>

## Knowledge as an Obligation

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim" (Ibn Majah). This obligation divides into two categories:

**First: Individual Obligation (Fard 'Ayn):** That which no Muslim may be ignorant of — the essential rulings of the religion, the pillars of faith and Islam, and what validates worship and transactions. This type is obligatory upon every accountable person individually.

**Second: Collective Obligation (Fard Kifayah):** That which suffices if undertaken by some Muslims and falls from the rest — such as specialized religious sciences and fields needed by the community. If enough people undertake it, the sin falls from all; if all neglect it, all bear the sin.

This distinction is fundamental to the fiqh of priorities, as it determines what the student begins with and what may be deferred.

## The Classification of Sciences in Islamic Heritage

Scholars have classified the sciences in various ways across the ages. Among the most well-known:

**Classification One — By Purpose:**

- Purposive Sciences (maqasid): Sought for their own sake, such as theology, Qur'anic exegesis, hadith, and jurisprudence.
- Instrumental Sciences (alah): Sought for the sake of something else, such as grammar, morphology, rhetoric, and logic.

**Classification Two — By Legal Ruling:**

- Individual obligation: Required of every accountable person.
- Collective obligation: Sufficed by some.
- Recommended: Praiseworthy but not obligatory.

**Classification Three — By Source:**

- Revealed sciences: Derived from revelation and tradition.
- Rational sciences: Derived from reason and experience.

These classifications are not contradictory but complementary, each offering a different lens to help the student form a clear cognitive map.

<div class="callout callout-success">

**Benefit:** A sound understanding of the ranks of sciences helps the student order priorities and avoid preoccupying themselves with branches before roots, or instrumental before purposive sciences — or vice versa, depending on need.

</div>

## Qualities of the Seeker of Knowledge

Religious knowledge is not merely information acquisition — it is a mode of being. Key qualities include:

1. **Sincerity:** That one's intention be for Allah alone, not for reputation or status. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever seeks knowledge to argue with the foolish, to compete with scholars, or to attract people's attention, will have his place in the Fire."
2. **Patience:** The pursuit of knowledge is a long journey requiring perseverance. Knowledge is not acquired overnight but requires years of sustained effort.
3. **Humility:** Knowledge veils its possessor from arrogance. The more one knows, the more one feels their ignorance before the vastness of knowledge.
4. **Action:** Knowledge without action is like a fruitless tree. Allah says: ﴾O you who believe, why do you say what you do not do?﴿ [As-Saff: 2-3].

## Summary

The virtue and pursuit of knowledge are firmly established in Islamic law, with abundant evidence from the Qur'an, Sunnah, and the legacy of the righteous predecessors. The seeker must always remain aware that seeking knowledge is worship before acquisition, that the responsibility entailed by knowledge is immense, and that the most worthy investment of effort and time is beneficial knowledge that draws one closer to Allah and benefits people.

﴾Rabbee zidnee ilma﴿ — "My Lord, increase me in knowledge" [Ta-Ha: 114].

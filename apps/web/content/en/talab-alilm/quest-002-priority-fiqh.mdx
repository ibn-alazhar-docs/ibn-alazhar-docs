## Introduction

The fiqh of priorities is a discipline in its own right within Islamic law, and one of the most important things a student of knowledge must master before embarking on their studies. Not every science should be studied at once. Not every book is suitable as an introduction. Not every teacher suits every beginner. Rather, there is a precise order established by the righteous predecessors, and whoever bypasses it stumbles or goes astray.

This document presents the correct methodology for ordering priorities in seeking knowledge, grounded in the sayings and experiences of the scholars.

## Why Fiqh of Priorities?

The need for priority fiqh arises from several considerations:

**Consideration One — Shortness of Life, Vastness of Knowledge:** Imam Al-Shafi'i said: "Knowledge is a boundless sea. Take the best of everything." Human life is finite, while the sciences are infinite. One must choose what is most beneficial and urgent.

**Consideration Two — Gradual Learning:** Souls cannot bear knowledge all at once. Ali ibn Abi Talib said: "Speak to people according to what they know. Would you have Allah and His Messenger be belied?" Gradualism is a cosmic and religious norm.

**Consideration Three — Hierarchical Ranks of Sciences:** Some sciences are foundations for others; some are branches that require prior mastery of the foundations. Prioritising branches over roots is futile.

<div class="callout callout-info">

**Grounding:** The fiqh of priorities in seeking knowledge is not a modern innovation; it is distilled from the method of the early generations in receiving and imparting knowledge. Observe how the Companions would learn ten verses at a time and act upon them.

</div>

## The Golden Rule of Ordering

The rule agreed upon by scholars across the ages can be formulated as:

> **Begin with the most important, then the next; with foundations before branches; with agreed-upon matters before disputed ones; with universals before particulars.**

Its elaboration:

1. **The most important first:** Prioritise what you need to preserve your faith and fulfil your obligations. Sound creed before differences in it. Obligatory jurisprudence before supererogatory knowledge.

2. **Roots before branches:** Learn the principles of exegesis before delving into exegesis, the principles of jurisprudence before jurisprudence, hadith terminology before hadith. Building without a foundation leads to collapse.

3. **Agreed-upon before disputed:** Start with settled matters upon which Ahl al-Sunnah agree, then examine matters of difference. Do not begin your intellectual life with disputes.

4. **Universals before particulars:** Master the general rules before the details. Ibn al-Qayyim said: "Part of a person's fiqh is knowing the universals under which the particulars fall."

## Where Does the Student Begin?

Based on the above, a suggested plan for the beginning student is:

**Stage One — Foundation:**

- Correct creed and tawhid
- Memorisation and understanding of the Qur'an
- Learning the rulings of purification, prayer, and essential worship
- Reading in prophetic biography and Islamic history

**Stage Two — Scientific Building:**

- Studying the principles of instrumental sciences (grammar, morphology)
- Studying principles of exegesis and Qur'anic sciences
- Studying hadith terminology and principles of jurisprudence
- Memorising core texts (mutun) in each discipline

**Stage Three — Expansion and Depth:**

- Reading detailed commentaries and major works
- Studying juristic differences and comparative fiqh
- Examining schools of thought and sects
- Specialising in one discipline

<div class="callout callout-warning">

**Note:** These stages are not rigid but overlapping. They may vary according to the student's circumstances, environment, and capacity. What matters is adherence to the general method.

</div>

## Common Errors in Prioritisation

Common mistakes students make:

1. **Occupying oneself with disputes before consensus:** Engaging with books of debate before mastering settled principles.
2. **Seeking knowledge for worldly gain:** Pursuing knowledge for employment or status rather than worship.
3. **Flitting between sciences without mastery:** Reading a little in every field without mastering any.
4. **Wasting time on introductions:** Spending excessive time on historical and theoretical introductions before entering the core of a discipline.
5. **Neglecting instrumental sciences:** Dismissing grammar, morphology, and rhetoric as useless, then struggling to understand sacred texts.

## Summary

The fiqh of priorities is the student's compass. Whoever masters it walks a straight path; whoever neglects it wanders lost. It has been said: "Part of a person's wisdom before entering into knowledge is knowing where to take knowledge from." This is the correct start for any successful scholarly journey.

## Introduction

Authorship and classification represent the crowning achievement of the student's trajectory. Through them, knowledge is preserved, benefit spreads, and legacy endures. Al-Azhar, over a millennium, has produced thousands of authors and compilers who enriched the Islamic library with foundational works in every discipline.

This document surveys the methods of authorship among Azhari scholars, presents examples of major encyclopaedias and compilations, and establishes a systematic methodology for sound scholarly writing.

## Types of Authorship in Azhari Heritage

Scholars distinguish between several types of authorship, each with its own method and characteristics:

**Type One — Encyclopaedias and Compendia:** Books aiming to gather the issues of one or multiple disciplines in a comprehensive work. Examples include "Al-Mawsu'ah al-Fiqhiyyah al-Kuwaitiyyah" (modern) and Ibn Abidin's "Radd al-Muhtar" which compiled Hanafi jurisprudence.

**Type Two — Commentaries and Glosses (Shuruh and Hawashi):** Books explaining a core text and elucidating its complexities. Al-Azhar is renowned for this type. Examples: al-Ushmuni's commentary on Alfiyyat Ibn Malik in grammar, and al-Bujayrimi's gloss on al-Khatib's commentary in jurisprudence.

**Type Three — Critical Editions (Tahqiq):** Extracting heritage works from manuscripts and publishing them in critical editions with annotation. This type has blossomed in the modern era with advances in printing.

**Type Four — Educational Texts and Abridgements (Mukhtasarat):** Books written specifically for students, characterised by simplicity and brevity. Examples: Matn Abi Shuja' in Shafi'i jurisprudence and al-Ajurrumiyya in grammar.

**Type Five — Scholarly Programmes and Chains (Baramij and Mashyakhat):** Works documenting a scholar's journey in seeking knowledge, listing their teachers and transmissions. These are important for preserving chains of transmission.

|------|---------|---------|
| Encyclopaedia | Comprehensive compilation | Al-Mawsu'ah al-Fiqhiyyah |
| Commentary | Elucidation of texts | Al-Ushmuni's commentary on Alfiyyah |
| Critical Edition | Heritage preservation | Critical edition of Tafsir al-Tabari |
| Abridgement | Pedagogy | Matn Abi Shuja' |
| Programme | Chain documentation | Wali al-Din al-Iraqi's "Barnamaj" |

## The Method of Sound Scholarly Authorship

Writing a rigorous scholarly book requires the following stages:

**Stage One — Topic Selection:** The topic should have scholarly value, not be exhaustively covered, and be needed by students. Ibn al-Jawzi said: "The scholar should not occupy himself with what does not concern him in his writings."

**Stage Two — Material Collection:** Gather texts from primary, not secondary, sources. Return to the foundational works (ummahat al-kutub) in each discipline.

**Stage Three — Organisation and Structuring:** Arrange material into well-ordered chapters and sections. A good structure enables the reader to find what they need easily.

**Stage Four — Composition and Editing:** Write in a clear, sound style, free of linguistic and scholarly errors. Review the text multiple times, each time with a different purpose (linguistic, scholarly, methodological).

**Stage Five — Review and Peer Assessment:** Present the work to specialists before publication. Incorporate their feedback.

**Stage Six — Publication and Distribution:** After final review, publish and distribute so that students may benefit.

**Counsell for Authors:** Ibn Khaldun said: "Authorship is an arduous craft, requiring great patience, sustained thought, and knowledge of writing methods and human nature." Do not rush to publish before the work matures.

## Major Azhari Works

Across Azhari history, major works continue to be studied and edited:

**In Jurisprudence:**

- "Radd al-Muhtar" by Ibn Abidin: An encyclopaedia of Hanafi jurisprudence, among the most important references.
- "Tuhfat al-Muhtaj" by Ibn Hajar al-Haytami: Commentary on al-Nawawi's Minhaj al-Talibin in Shafi'i jurisprudence.
- "Hashiyat al-Dasuqi" on the great commentary in Maliki jurisprudence.

**In Exegesis and Qur'anic Sciences:**

- "Tafsir al-Jalalayn" by al-Suyuti and al-Mahalli: Among the most popular concise exegeses.
- "Al-Burhan fi Ulum al-Qur'an" by al-Zarkashi: A foundational source in Qur'anic sciences.

**In Language and Grammar:**

- "Mughni al-Labib" by Ibn Hisham al-Ansari: One of the greatest works in Arabic grammar.
- "Sharh Ibn Aqil" on Alfiyyat Ibn Malik: Among the most famous pedagogical commentaries.

## The Future of Authorship in the Digital Age

The digital age presents both challenges and opportunities:

1. **Digital encyclopaedias:** The possibility of building digital scholarly works surpassing printed ones in searchability and access speed.
2. **Collaborative authorship:** Platforms enabling multiple scholars to contribute to a single work.
3. **Digital verification:** AI tools for text verification and citation checking.
4. **Open access:** Making books available to all without financial barriers.

Yet the fundamental principles of sound scholarly authorship remain unchanged: accuracy, intellectual honesty, mastery, and service to truth.

## Summary

Authorship and classification represent the summit of scholarly work. Through them, the scholar's legacy endures and their benefit spreads after death. Al-Azhar over a millennium has produced a vast wealth of compilations that form the backbone of the Islamic library. The new generation of students must draw inspiration from this rich tradition while adding what suits the age.

"The best of people are those most beneficial to others." And beneficial authorship is among the greatest forms of benefit, with the most lasting impact.

## Introduction

Sacred knowledge is not merely information stored in the mind or inscribed in notebooks — it is light that Allah casts into the heart, and a mode of being that manifests in the life of its possessor. For this reason, the righteous predecessors gave extraordinary attention to etiquette (adab), prioritising it over the acquisition of knowledge itself. Imam Malik said to a youth from Quraysh: "Learn etiquette before you learn knowledge."

This document presents a distillation of what the scholars have said concerning the etiquette of the seeker of knowledge toward themselves, their teacher, their books, and their time.

## Etiquette Toward Oneself

**First: Sincerity of Intention:** This is the foundation of acceptance. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Actions are but by intentions, and each person will have but what they intended." Sincere intention elevates the pursuit of knowledge from a habit to an act of worship. Signs of sincerity: not seeking status, wealth, or reputation through knowledge.

**Second: Acting Upon Knowledge:** One of the most dangerous afflictions is knowing without acting. Allah says: ﴾O you who believe, why do you say what you do not do?﴿ [Al-Saff: 3]. The early generations, when they learned a chapter of knowledge, would act upon it before learning the next.

**Third: Humility:** Knowledge begets humility, not arrogance. Ibn al-Qayyim said: "Whoever humbles themselves before Allah, He raises them." Part of the student's humility: learning from those younger or less prominent, and not being too proud to ask about what they do not know.

**Fourth: Patience and Fortitude:** The path of knowledge is long and arduous. Imam Ahmad said: "I accompanied al-Shafi'i and never saw anyone more eager for knowledge. He would seek knowledge even during illness."

## Etiquette Toward the Teacher

Knowledge is taken from the mouths of men, not solely from the pages of books. Receiving from teachers has its own etiquette:

1. **Choose well:** Select a teacher who is learned and practicing, of good character, and well-regarded for virtue. Ibn Sirin said: "This knowledge is religion — watch from whom you take your religion."

2. **Good conduct in the gathering:** Do not raise your voice above the teacher's, do not interrupt, do not speak before them, and do not contradict them. The early generations would sit before their teachers as if birds were perched on their heads.

3. **Active listening:** Listen to the teacher with heart before ear. Do not occupy yourself with anything other than what the teacher presents. Record benefits you hear.

4. **Patience with the teacher's occasional harshness:** No scholar is without moments of sharpness. Ibn Jama'ah said: "Whoever does not endure the bitterness of learning and teaching for an hour will bear the humiliation of ignorance for a lifetime."

5. **Gratitude and acknowledgment:** Al-Shafi'i said: "There is no one to whom I am not indebted, except Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i himself."

## Etiquette Toward Books

The book is the vessel of knowledge, with its own sanctity and etiquette:

1. **Choose wisely:** Consult trustworthy people before purchasing or reading a book. Choose the best commentaries and editions.

2. **Read gradually:** Begin with concise texts, progress to intermediate ones, then to detailed works. Do not read a book before you are prepared to understand it.

3. **Understand what you read:** Do not be like a wood-gatherer at night, collecting everything you see without understanding. Pause at each point until you absorb it.

4. **Engage with the book:** Annotate margins, extract benefits, and reread. Al-Khatib al-Baghdadi said: "Knowledge will not give you a part of it until you give it all of you."

5. **Physical care:** Preserve the book from loss and damage.

## Etiquette Toward Time

Time is the student's capital, and wasting it is irredeemable loss:

1. **Seize the moment:** The early generations guarded every moment. Ibn al-Jawzi said: "I have seen nothing more harmful to the student of knowledge than wasting time."

2. **Balance worship, study, and rest:** Do not overpower one aspect at the expense of another. Your body has a right over you, your eyes have a right, and your family has a right.

3. **Avoid excessive permissible activities:** Too many permissible diversions distract from knowledge.

4. **Harness peak hours:** Each person has times of greater alertness and retention. Reserve these for the most difficult and subtle matters.

## Summary

Etiquette in seeking knowledge is like the soul to the body. Knowledge without etiquette is like a weapon in the hands of a madman. Al-Shafi'i expressed it beautifully:

> "Whoever learns knowledge without disciplining his soul is like one who grips a sword by its blade — its edge cuts him without him feeling it."

The etiquette of the seeker of knowledge is neither luxury nor ornament — it lies at the very core of seeking knowledge and is fundamental to its acceptance.
